WARTHOG

Phacochoerus is a genus of wild pigs in the family Suidae, known as warthogs. They are found in open and semiopen habitats, even in quite arid regions, in sub-Saharan Africa. The two species were formerly considered conspecific under the scientific name Phacochoerus aethiopicus.

Warthogs are identifiable by the two pairs of tusks protruding from the mouth and curving upwards. The lower pair, which is far shorter than the upper pair, becomes razor-sharp by rubbing against the upper pair every time the mouth is opened and closed. The upper canine teeth can grow to 25.5 cm (10 in) long.

Although they are capable of fighting (males fight each other during mating season), warthogs prefer to flee when threatened. They are fast runners and can run up to 48 km/h (30 mph). They will often reverse into burrows so they can defend themselves with their tusks if pursued.

Did You Know?

  • Warthogs can go months without water during dry seasons
  • They often kneel on their calloused, hairy, padded knees to eat
  • Warthogs allow banded mongooses to groom them for ticks
